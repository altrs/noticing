Birds on a wire - Living Atop Infrastructure

Upon reading ‘How to See Infrastructure’ and ‘Networks of New York’, it was very interesting to learn more about the various metal boxes, poles, markings, grates, manholes, etc. that I walk past everyday. Upon learning more about them from this class’s readings, I became interested in all that surrounded these objects, and how we’ve come to exist on top of these structures that support us. For this project, I am specifically interested in the dissonance between what these objects are meant for, and how passer-byers instead choose to use them — possibly unaware or uncaring of what the street object is. Sometimes it seems we are all just birds on wires.

In my documentation, I focused on looking for examples of such phenomena where we somehow exist on top of these physical structures: people using a fire hydrant as a seat, poles as ‘message boards’ for posters and stickers, etc. These structures exist in our world going largely unnoticed, yet used unconciously in a multitude of ways. The same ways we often disregard infrastructure as a whole, we also disregard its physical ‘residue.’

I attempted to do research on urban/street infrastructure enclosures, but, not surprisingly, not many articles were found — further suggesting our collective overlooking of this topic. However, there was one article that discusses ‘urban surfaces’ which “encompasses all the surfaces that characterize physically and morphologically the built environment from the radiative, thermal, and hydrological perspective.” Thinking about the obvious importance of the structural integrity of these urban structures and surfaces — their placement and form have arguably created ‘by-product uses.’
